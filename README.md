## 📘 Overview

The **Algorithm of Sustainability (AoS)** is a modular, cybernetic economic framework designed to stabilize monetary systems, reduce structural inequality, and align economic activity with ecological boundaries.  
It integrates four core mechanisms—**Liquidity Levy (LL)**, **Land Value Levy (LVL)**, **Land Value Tax (LVT)**, and **Universal Dividend (UD)**—into a coherent, rule‑based operating system for sustainable economic governance.

AoS is intended as an open scientific framework for interdisciplinary research in economics, systems theory, public policy, and ecological governance.

## 🔧 Core Mechanisms

AoS consists of four interacting components that form a closed feedback system:

### 1. Liquidity Levy (LL)
A continuous demurrage‑like fee on idle monetary balances.

**Research relevance:**  
- Stabilizes liquidity cycles  
- Reduces speculative hoarding  
- Prevents deflationary spirals  
- Enables counter‑cyclical self‑regulation  

### 2. Land Value Levy (LVL)
A levy on community‑created land and resource rents.

**Research relevance:**  
- Captures unearned rents  
- Reduces real‑estate speculation  
- Supports efficient land allocation  
- Internalizes spatial externalities  

### 3. Land Value Tax (LVT)
A structural tax reform replacing taxes on labor and productive activity with taxes on land value.

**Research relevance:**  
- Removes distortions in labor markets  
- Increases economic efficiency  
- Reduces rent‑seeking  
- Simplifies fiscal systems  

### 4. Universal Dividend (UD)
A periodic, equal distribution of all LL + LVL revenues to the population.

**Research relevance:**  
- Provides a stable income floor  
- Reduces inequality without bureaucracy  
- Strengthens aggregate demand  
- Compensates for resource‑efficient lifestyles

## 🔄 System Architecture

AoS is built as a cybernetic control system:

- **LL** stabilizes the monetary subsystem  
- **LVL** and **LVT** stabilize the resource and land subsystem  
- **UD** stabilizes the social subsystem  

Together, they form a self‑correcting macroeconomic architecture that reduces the need for discretionary intervention.
